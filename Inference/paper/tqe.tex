\section{Background}%1-1.5 pages
In this section we aim at introducing the background required to understand the proposed method, focusing on the Ising Model and its realization through quantum annealing, as well as the learning process the method is based on.

\subsection{The Ising Model}
The Ising model provides a mathematical framework originally introduced to describe ferromagnetic systems. It is defined on a graph \( (V, E) \), where each vertex \( i \in V \) represents a discrete spin variable \( z_i \in \{-1, +1\} \). Each spin is associated with a local bias \( \theta_i \in \mathbb{R} \) and pairwise couplings \( \Gamma_{ij} \in \mathbb{R} \) for each edge \( (i, j) \). The total energy of a configuration is given by the Hamiltonian:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ising}
    E(\theta, \Gamma, z) = \sum_{i \in V} \theta_i z_i + \sum_{(i,j) \in E} \Gamma_{ij} z_i z_j.
\end{equation} 

An Ising machine seeks the spin configuration that minimizes this Hamiltonian:
\begin{equation}
z^* = \arg\min_z E(\theta, \Gamma, z),
\end{equation}
where $z^*$ corresponds to the ground state configuration. This minimization process can be used to encode a wide class of combinatorial optimization problems, since many discrete problems can be expressed in the form of the Ising model.

\subsection{Quantum Annealing}
Quantum annealing is a heuristic method for solving optimization problems by evolving a quantum system toward the ground state of a problem Hamiltonian. 
In the context of the Ising model, the optimization problem defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:ising} can be mapped to the ground-state search of a corresponding Hamiltonian.
Specifically, consider the time-dependent Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
    H(t) = \gamma(t) H_D + H_P
\end{equation}
where $H_P$ is the problem Hamiltonian, $H_D$ is the transverse field Hamiltonian, and $\gamma : \mathbb{R}^+ \to \mathbb{R}$ is a decreasing function that gradually suppresses the kinetic term $H_D$, guiding the system toward the ground state of $H_P$. The transverse field Hamiltonian $H_D$ provides quantum fluctuations that allow the exploration of the solution landscape, while the modulation $\gamma(t)$ controls the annealing process.  

Quantum annealing can be physically realized using a network of qubits arranged on the vertices of a graph $(V, E)$, with $|V| = n$, where the edges $E$ represent the couplings between qubits. The problem Hamiltonian is defined as a self-adjoint operator on the $n$-qubit Hilbert space $\left(\mathbb{C}^2\right)^{\otimes n}$:
\begin{equation}
H_P = \sum_{i \in V} \theta_i \, \sigma_z^{(i)} + \sum_{(i,j) \in E} \Gamma_{ij} \, \sigma_z^{(i)} \sigma_z^{(j)},
\label{hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_z^{(i)}$ is the Pauli-$z$ operator acting on qubit $i$, and $\theta_i$ and $\Gamma_{ij}$ represent, respectively, the local fields and the couplings between qubits.
Thus, quantum annealing can be viewed as a physical mechanism for minimizing the Ising Hamiltonian.

\subsection{Parametric Model}
The parametric model proposed by Schmid et al. \cite{10.21468/SciPostPhysCore.7.1.013} defines a supervised learning framework based on the principles of Ising machines.
The definition of the model is the following:
\begin{align}
F(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\Gamma, \lambda, \varepsilon) &= \lambda \min_{\mathbf{z} \in \{-1,1\}^n} E(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \Gamma, \mathbf{z}) + \varepsilon \\
&= \lambda E_0(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \Gamma) + \varepsilon.
\label{model}
\end{align}
In this formulation, input data are encoded into the spin biases $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$ of an Ising model, while the trainable parameters correspond to the couplings $\Gamma_{ij}$ determined by the topology of the underlying Ising machine. The model output is defined as the ground-state energy of the system, scaled and shifted through two additional parameters $\lambda$ and $\varepsilon$.

\subsubsection{Training Phase}

Given the training set $\mathcal{D} = \{(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(a)}, y^{(a)})\}_{a=1,\ldots,N}$, with $y^{(a)} = f(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(a)})$, where $f : \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is an unknown function to approximate, the model in \ref{model} can be trained by minimizing a loss function, for example the mean squared error (MSE).
\begin{equation}
L(\Gamma, \lambda, \varepsilon) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{a=1}^{N} 
\left[ F(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(a)} | \Gamma, \lambda, \varepsilon) - y^{(a)} \right]^2 
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
The objective is to perform this minimization task by using a gradient descent approach, iteratively updating the parameters $\Gamma$, $\lambda$, and $\varepsilon$ by taking steps in the direction opposite to the gradient of the loss function $L$:
\begin{equation}
\delta \Gamma = -\eta \nabla_{\Gamma} L, \quad
\delta \lambda = -\eta \frac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda}, \quad
\delta \varepsilon = -\eta \frac{\partial L}{\partial \varepsilon},
\label{eq:gradients}
\end{equation}
where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate controlling the optimization step size.


This representation highlights a neural-like structure, where spins act as neurons and couplings as synaptic weights. Notably, the learning process does not require exact ground-state solutions; approximate energies produced by the Ising machine can be effectively integrated into the optimization, as their systematic deviations are absorbed by the parameter tuning process. This property makes the model practical and robust for real-world training scenarios.

\section{Proposal}
\subsection{Quantum Ising Modules in Neural Networks}

Integrating quantum-inspired optimization models within neural network architectures opens new avenues for solving complex, high-dimensional problems. In this work, we propose a framework where quantum Ising models are embedded as differentiable modules within a neural network, combining the combinatorial optimization capabilities of Ising machines with the function approximation power of deep learning.

\subsubsection{Motivation}

Standard neural networks rely on continuous differentiable mappings to propagate gradients during training. However, many real-world optimization problems are discrete in nature and can be naturally expressed as spin systems in the form of Ising Hamiltonians. Quantum annealers, or their classical simulations, provide a mechanism to efficiently explore the combinatorial space and approximate ground-state solutions of these Hamiltonians. Embedding such mechanisms into neural networks allows us to leverage this optimization power while retaining end-to-end differentiability for gradient-based learning.

\subsubsection{Differentiable Ising Energy Function}

To integrate an Ising-based module within a neural network, we define a custom differentiable function that computes the ground-state energy of an Ising Hamiltonian given a batch of input biases. For each input vector $\theta$, representing a set of local fields on the spins, the module uses an annealing solver to find an approximate ground-state configuration \(z^*\) that minimizes the Hamiltonian:

\[
E_0(\theta, \Gamma) = \min_{z \in \{-1,+1\}^n} \left( \sum_i \theta_i z_i + \sum_{i<j} \Gamma_{ij} z_i z_j \right),
\]

where $\Gamma$ are the learnable couplings encoding the interactions between spins. By storing the resulting spin configurations during the forward pass, the module can propagate gradients with respect to $\Gamma$ in the backward pass using a surrogate gradient:

\[
\frac{\partial E_0}{\partial \Gamma_{ij}} = z_i^* z_j^*.
\]

This design ensures that the module is fully compatible with automatic differentiation frameworks such as PyTorch, allowing seamless integration with larger neural network architectures.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Differentiable Ising Energy Function (Forward and Backward)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} batch of biases $\{\theta_i\}$, coupling matrix $\Gamma$, annealer instance
\STATE \textbf{Forward pass:}
\FOR{each sample $i$ in batch}
    \STATE Convert $\theta_i$ to biases $h_i$
    \STATE Convert $\Gamma$ to coupling matrix $J$
    \STATE Sample approximate ground state $z_i^*$ using annealer
    \STATE Compute energy $E_i = \sum_j h_{i,j} z_{i,j} + \sum_{j<k} J_{jk} z_{i,j} z_{i,k}$
    \STATE Store $z_i^*$ for backward
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Return:} batch energies $\{E_i\}$
\STATEx
\STATE \textbf{Backward pass:}
\FOR{each sample $i$ in batch}
    \STATE Compute outer product $z_i^* (z_i^*)^\top$
    \STATE Multiply by incoming gradient $\partial L / \partial E_i$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Average over batch and retain upper-triangular part to get $\partial L / \partial \Gamma$
\STATE \textbf{Return:} $\partial L / \partial \Gamma$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Full Ising Module}

While the \texttt{IsingLayer} computes the Ising ground-state energy, the \texttt{FullIsingModule} extends this functionality by introducing two additional learnable parameters: a scaling factor $\lambda$ and an energy offset. The module output is defined as:

\[
E_\text{out} = \lambda E_0 + \text{offset}.
\]

This linear transformation allows the Ising module to match the scale of the target outputs in a supervised learning context, providing flexibility analogous to the activation and bias terms in traditional neural network layers. Consequently, the module can be trained end-to-end, learning both the spin couplings and the  scaling parameters.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Forward Pass of Full Ising Module}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Compute $E_0$ using Ising Energy Function
\STATE Scale and shift: $E_\text{out} = \lambda \cdot E_0 + \text{offset}$
\STATE \textbf{Return:} $E_\text{out}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Parallelized Computation and Scalability}

The computation of ground-state energies for a batch of inputs can be efficiently parallelized. By splitting the batch across multiple threads, each thread computes the annealing solution for a subset of inputs. This allows leveraging multicore CPUs or distributed systems for scalability, ensuring that the computational overhead of embedding an Ising solver within a neural network remains manageable even for moderately large spin systems.

\subsubsection{Integration into Neural Networks}
Embedding the Full Ising Module in a neural network effectively creates a hybrid model where discrete combinatorial optimization is combined with continuous learning. The neural network can supply input biases to the Ising module, which then computes a structured, discrete representation of the energy landscape. Gradients flow backward from the loss function through the Ising module to update both the couplings and the scaling parameters. This enables the network to learn complex mappings that require both discrete reasoning and continuous function approximation.

\subsubsection{Single-Layer Ising Neural Network}\label{sec:singlelayer}
In addition to a single \texttt{FullIsingModule}, we consider a shallow architecture composed of $M$ parallel Full Ising Modules whose outputs are linearly combined. This structure can be interpreted as a single-layer neural network where each unit is an independent Ising-based energy model.

Given an input vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, the $m$-th \texttt{FullIsingModule} produces an output
\begin{equation}
F^{(m)}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \lambda^{(m)} \, E_0\!\big(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \Gamma^{(m)}\big) \;+\; \varepsilon^{(m)},
\label{eq:fullising_out}
\end{equation}
where $E_0(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \Gamma^{(m)})$ denotes the ground-state energy of the Ising Hamiltonian with input biases $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ and trainable couplings $\Gamma^{(m)}$, while $\lambda^{(m)}$ and $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ are learnable scaling and offset parameters.

The single-layer network with $M$ parallel modules aggregates their outputs through a linear readout:
\begin{equation}
F(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; b \;+\; \sum_{m=1}^{M} w_m \, F^{(m)}(\boldsymbol{\theta}),
\label{eq:linear_comb_fullising}
\end{equation}
with $w_m$ and $b$ being additional trainable parameters.

This architecture is analogous to a single fully connected layer in standard neural networks, with the key difference that each neuron is replaced by a structured Ising energy minimization block. The nonlinearity arises from the discrete ground-state computation within each module, while the outer linear combination enables flexible aggregation of multiple energy-based representations.

Despite its shallow structure, this model increases expressive power compared to a single Ising module, as different modules can learn different coupling patterns, effectively acting as an ensemble of energy-based learners trained end-to-end.

\subsubsection{Datasets}

The experimental evaluation was conducted on a broad selection of benchmark
datasets obtained from the UCI Machine Learning
Repository~\cite{Dua:2019}. The considered datasets span different feature
dimensionalities and sample sizes in order to assess the robustness and
generalization capabilities of the proposed approach across heterogeneous
scenarios.

The UCI datasets are stored in their original, possibly imbalanced form
(see Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). Balancing is enforced at evaluation
time only: we adopt stratified 5-fold cross-validation and, within each
fold, we apply random undersampling of the majority class independently
on the training and the test split. As a result, every training fold
contains an equal number of class~0 and class~1 samples, and the same
holds for every test fold, so that both training and evaluation operate
on perfectly balanced data. Feature standardization was performed by
computing the mean and standard deviation exclusively on the training
set, and subsequently applying the same transformation to the test set.
This procedure prevents information leakage and ensures a fair evaluation
protocol.

In addition to real-world benchmarks, we conducted controlled experiments on
synthetic multidimensional XOR problems, ranging from 1-D to 6-D. These
datasets were included to explicitly evaluate the capability of the models
to capture higher-order non-linear interactions.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Model performance was assessed using 5-fold cross-validation ($k=5$). The
random seed was fixed across all experiments to ensure reproducibility. For
each fold, training and validation splits were generated deterministically,
and final performance metrics were computed by averaging results across folds.

The following evaluation metrics were collected:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Classification accuracy,
    \item Precision,
    \item Recall,
    \item $F_1$ score,
    \item Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC).
\end{itemize}

These metrics provide a comprehensive assessment of classification
performance, including sensitivity to class-specific errors.

\subsubsection{Training and Inference Procedure}

The training phase was implemented using PyTorch. All models were trained
using the Adam optimizer and the Binary Cross-Entropy loss with logits
(\texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss}). Models were trained for a fixed number of
epochs with mini-batch gradient descent. During inference, the sigmoid
activation was applied to the logits to obtain class probabilities, and a
threshold of 0.5 was used for binary classification.

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Configuration}

To ensure a fair comparison between the considered architectures, the same
set of hyperparameters was adopted across all models. These hyperparameters
were not individually optimized for each architecture and dataset; instead,
they were selected to provide reasonably good and stable performance across
the considered benchmarks. This choice allows the comparison to focus on
architectural differences rather than on architecture-specific hyperparameter
tuning.
